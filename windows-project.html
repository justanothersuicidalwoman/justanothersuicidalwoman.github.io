Windows Project

Waiting for inspiration is what holds writing back from being born. Days can slip by faster than imagined without a single word written, simply because we cannot find the "right" topic or the "perfect" circumstances. In the method of uncreative writing, two things become clear: first, there is no more time to wait. Writing is a practice, and practice requires a structural container to happen—even in the absence of ideas. Second, there is already a massive surplus of material surrounding us from which we can craft text; sometimes we do not need to invent anything new, but simply work with what already exists. I used to be a shopping addict, but today, shopping centers, malls, and "stuff—shitloads of stuff" only make me sick. Instead, I have become a collector. And, still, a writer. Collecting and writing are the fundamental tools of uncreative writing. In this project, I attempt to extract text directly from the city—specifically from the windowpanes of houses in the Netherlands—turning the physical cityscape into the pages of a book. Uncreatively inspired by Walter Benjamin’s Arcades Project, Kenneth Goldsmith’s Uncreative Writing, and Marjorie Perloff’s Unoriginal Genius, this work is a refusal to create, a poetry of what already exists, and an absolute commitment to writing as an active practice.

This project is activated as an open, inclusive workshop designed for everyone—from children and adults to practicing artists. We begin with a brief introduction exploring how writing evolves across different eras, tracing the structural shifts brought on first by the rise of the web and now by the rise of AI. Through this lens, we discover how uncreative writing serves as a powerful tool to re-evaluate our own subjectivity and creativity by working with what is already there. The hands-on process then begins with a collective acts of observation: if we are inside, we look out through the windows; if we are outside, we peer in. We actively collect the text present in our environment—whether it is literally written on surfaces or simply described from what we see. Together, the participants construct an "imaginary window," using it to generate uncreative writings or creative writing prompts from our gathered material. By selecting the fragments, sentences, and visual combinations that resonate with us, we establish our own starting points. From there, participants dive into writing: whether through automatic writing, creative prose, or collaborative, collective text - using the existing world as our ultimate raw material.
